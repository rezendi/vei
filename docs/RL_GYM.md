# RL Gym: Scoped RL Environment on VEI

## Objective

This document describes a defensible, scoped path for using VEI as an RL environment. It is the result of an explicit narrowing exercise: we started from the maximalist framing — VEI as "a programmable replica of a company's operational stack" suitable for general agent training — and worked backward, removing claims that did not survive scrutiny, until we reached a version that does.

The objective is to use VEI to RL-train agents on **process correctness and compliance**, where correctness is expressible as deterministic predicates over event-spine state. This is RL for *process*, not RL for *outcomes*. The agent learns to follow well-specified procedural and governance properties: cross-surface referential integrity, permission and scope adherence, audit-trail completeness, idempotency under retry, required-step coverage, schema validity on tool calls. The agent does not learn to make business judgments, and the trained policy makes no claims about real-world business performance. The narrower scope is what allows the rigor claims to hold.

## Why this scoping holds up

VEI's defining technical property is a deterministic, snapshot/branch/replayable kernel with a typed event spine. That property is genuinely valuable: it gives us reproducible trajectories, clean credit assignment via replay, and exact A/B counterfactuals at fixed seeds. These are properties most enterprise-facing simulators do not have, and they make VEI a credible substrate for some kinds of training.

But the same determinism that makes VEI a good evaluation harness is a liability for training. The world that VEI presents to an agent is generated by templates, seeded RNG, and regex predicates. An RL agent is a reward-maximizer that does not care whether the reward signal is "real," and any seam in the world generation — templated vendor reply distributions, regex-based approval checks, fixed-content NPC scripts, pristine well-tagged CRM rows — is a free reward channel for the policy to exploit. The richer and more structured the simulated content, the more places those exploits hide.

Compounding this, the deterministic content layer is unrealistic in a way that matters. Real Jira is not a state machine; it is a corpus of tickets written and routed by tired humans whose tagging is inconsistent and whose priority decisions are partly political. VEI faithfully simulates the API contract of every surface and replaces the human-content layer with seeded determinism. For evaluation that is often acceptable, because one can construct evaluation cases that do not depend on content-layer realism. For training, it is not, because the policy will optimize against whatever distribution it sees, including the distribution of clean, well-formed content that does not exist outside demo environments.

The way out of this is to recognize that not every property an agent might learn is content-distribution-sensitive. Process correctness is the category whose verifier is mechanical: a deterministic predicate over event-spine state. "Did the agent attempt action X without role Y?" is true or false regardless of how realistic the world's content is. "Was the audit trail captured for every privileged action?" likewise. These predicates are robust to content-layer fiction because they do not depend on content-layer realism — they depend on the mechanism layer, which VEI faithfully implements.

So the defensible RL surface is the set of agent properties that can be expressed as **complete specifications via deterministic predicates over event-spine state**. RL on these produces agents that are better at the verified properties, in a way that can be reported with calibration claims that survive scrutiny.

The qualifier "complete specification" is load-bearing. A predicate that *is* the property is RL-safe. A predicate that is a *proxy* for the property is RL-poison: the policy will satisfy the proxy in ways the contract author did not anticipate, and Goodhart will eat the trained model. The discipline required is being honest about which contracts are complete specs and which are proxies pretending to be complete specs. Most of the engineering risk in this program lives in the spec-authoring step, not in the training loop.

## Cross-check via Enron

The one place VEI has real-world ground truth is the Enron archive. The archive is unsuitable as a primary training distribution for this RL program — it is one company in one industry with idiosyncratic dysfunction — but it is well suited as a sanity check on process training, because it contains real trajectories of both compliant and non-compliant historical behavior.

The cross-check works as follows. After process training, replay the trained agent through Enron archive branch points and ask whether its behavior distribution clusters with compliant historical actors (legal review chains followed, escalation timing reasonable, attachment handling within scope) or with non-compliant ones (off-record coordination, deliberate escalation avoidance, document-destruction patterns). If a "compliance-trained" agent participates in off-record coordination on archive replay, the training is broken in a way the in-VEI metrics did not catch. If the agent's behavior is statistically distinct from the non-compliant historical distribution along the dimensions the contracts were designed to teach, that is real evidence the contracts are capturing something beyond their own specification.

This sidesteps the counterfactual-ground-truth problem that limits other parts of VEI's eval framework. We are not asking the agent for counterfactual judgment; we are asking whether its process trace, on real archive timelines, distributionally matches the compliant historical exemplars. The archive provides distribution, not ground truth on individual decisions, and that is enough for a sanity check though not enough for headline performance claims. The Enron result should always be reported as a cross-check, never as the primary metric.

## Plan: building the contract-only RL environment

### Stage 1 — Scope the contract surface

We pick a narrow, well-bounded set of compliance and correctness properties to start. The first cohort should be properties for which a complete deterministic specification is genuinely possible, violations are common in untrained agent behavior, and the predicates can be evaluated cheaply on replay. Candidate properties include cross-surface referential integrity (every doc-referenced ticket exists, every ticket-assigned user exists in identity), scope and permission adherence (every action falls within the declared mission scope and the agent's role), audit-trail completeness (every privileged action has actor, timestamp, justification captured), idempotency under retry, and required-step coverage (resolution posted before ticket closure, approval queued before destructive action). Each property gets one contract; no contract should try to specify two properties.

### Stage 2 — Author contracts as complete specifications with multi-author agreement

Each contract is reviewed by at least two authors before it enters the training set. The review is not for whether the contract "looks right" but specifically for whether it is a complete spec of the property or a proxy that happens to correlate with it. Disagreements between authors are usually evidence that the property is fuzzier than it looked, in which case the right move is to split the property into narrower sub-properties or to drop it from the contract surface entirely. This is the one place humans are load-bearing in process RL, and it is one-time spec-authoring work, not ongoing per-trajectory calibration. The output of this stage is a set of contracts, each accompanied by a short rationale describing exactly what property it specifies and what it deliberately does not specify.

### Stage 3 — Build a diverse training scenario set

We need scenarios that genuinely exercise each contract property, including adversarial cases where the easy path violates the contract. Branch points should be constructed so that multiple actions are mechanically available but only a subset satisfy the contract. The existing vertical packs (service ops, B2B SaaS, real estate, marketing agency, storage logistics) can host these scenarios; we may also build a vertical specifically for compliance training, where every scenario maps cleanly to one or more contract properties. Diversity matters along several axes — surface coverage, scenario length, branch-point density — because narrow training corpora produce narrow agents.

### Stage 4 — Adversarial probe before training

Before any training run, run a small exploration policy against the candidate contracts and look for seams. Every regex approval check, templated vendor response, deterministic NPC script, and fixed-content lookup is a candidate exploit. Anywhere the small policy can satisfy the contract via a degenerate path that the author did not intend is a contract bug or a content bug, and those get fixed before scaling. This is a CI-style gate, not a one-time exercise: every contract change and every content change re-runs the probe. Without this gate, every later result is suspect, because the trained policy may simply have rediscovered the exploit at scale.

### Stage 5 — Training loop, starting conservative

The first training regime is rejection sampling on contract-passing traces. The policy generates trajectories, the contract layer verifies them, passing trajectories become SFT data, and the policy is fine-tuned on the filtered set. This is the most reward-hacking-resistant training method available because the policy is not actively optimizing against the verifier — it is being fine-tuned on examples that happened to pass. For the narrow process properties this program targets, rejection sampling is usually sufficient. Online RL with policy gradient is overkill, substantially more dangerous, and should not be the default approach.

If rejection sampling plateaus and a property is still not well-learned, the next step is preference learning (DPO or similar) on contract-pass-versus-contract-fail trajectory pairs. This is moderate risk because the policy is being optimized against pair preferences, but the preferences are mechanical — the contract is the labeler — so the failure mode is contract-specification gaming rather than judge-model gaming, which is easier to detect. Online RL with policy gradient is reserved for properties that demonstrably resist both rejection sampling and DPO, and only with continuous adversarial probing in the loop.

### Stage 6 — Held-out evaluation

We hold out at four levels: scenarios within a vertical, entire vertical packs, contract instances within a property class, and seed ranges within a scenario. The held-out evaluation set is frozen at experiment start and is never used for training-time selection. Reported metrics include per-property contract pass rate on held-out scenarios, per-vertical pass rate to detect overfitting, and degradation under varied seeds. A trained model that posts strong numbers on the training distribution but degrades sharply on a held-out vertical is overfitting to vertical idiosyncrasies, not learning the property; that pattern is the most common failure mode and the held-out cohort is what surfaces it.

### Stage 7 — Enron sanity check

On the trained policy, run replay through Enron archive branch points and compare the policy's behavior distribution against the compliant historical exemplar distribution and the non-compliant exemplar distribution. The sanity check passes if the policy's behavior is closer to the compliant distribution than to the non-compliant one along the dimensions the contracts were designed to teach, and in particular if the policy does not replicate non-compliant patterns even when those patterns satisfy short-term reward signals inside VEI. Reported as a cross-check, not as a headline metric, because Enron is one company with one specific dysfunction and over-fitting to its norms is its own failure mode.

### Stage 8 — Reproducibility hygiene

Every result row records seed, blueprint hash, contract-set hash, training-data hash, model-checkpoint hash, and (if applicable) judge-model id and prompt hash. The frozen held-out contract set is never modified during a research line; if it is modified, the line restarts. Without this discipline, reward drift across experiments silently invalidates comparisons, and the failure mode is invisible until someone tries to reproduce a result and cannot. This stage is administrative but non-optional.

## Future work: calibrated judge evals to expand the RL surface

The contract-only environment described above covers the *specifiable* portion of agent quality. There is a natural next ring of properties that are judgment-mediated but still reasonably specifiable: tone appropriateness given recipient seniority, escalation timing within process latitude, choice between multiple contract-satisfying actions, spirit-of-policy adherence. No deterministic predicate captures these cleanly, but a calibrated LLM judge could. Expanding into this surface would move training from "agent provably follows process" to "agent follows process and exercises reasonable judgment within the latitude process allows," which is a meaningful expansion. The audit-eval-calibration UI already shipping in VEI is the infrastructure for capturing the human alignment data the expansion would require.

Three prerequisites apply, however, and each is nontrivial. First, calibration must be measured per-property; a judge calibrated on enterprise-risk ranking is not necessarily calibrated on tone appropriateness, and calibration claims do not transfer across property classes. Second, calibration must be re-measured on policy-induced trajectory distributions, not just on the original held-out set, because RL against a judge is the standard way to discover what the judge can be fooled by and one-shot calibration on archive data degrades under distributional shift. Third, multi-rater data with measured inter-rater reliability — Cohen's kappa or Krippendorff's alpha — is required, because single-rater audits are calibration to one person's preferences and Goodhart routinely finds them.

If those prerequisites are met, the expanded surface admits RLAIF-style training against the calibrated judge as reward model, with the same staged caution as the contract-only path: rejection sampling first, preference learning next, online RL only where lower-risk methods plateau. Without those prerequisites, expanding into judge-mediated training is the reward-hacking trap dressed up in academic clothing, and the contract-only environment should remain the primary training surface.

The honest sequencing is therefore: build and validate the contract-only environment first, demonstrate it produces meaningful improvements on held-out compliance metrics and survives the Enron sanity check, and only then invest in the calibration apparatus that would let the surface expand. Trying to build both at once produces neither.
